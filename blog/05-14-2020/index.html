One Universe, Infinite

written by Joe Berusch on 2020-05-14

Today I learned that some physicists believe in a "many worlds" theory of quantum mechanics... and it's insane.

I'm going to butcher this theory, but here goes. These physicists believe that every time something is observed, the universe splits off into fragments, one for each of the possible ways that thing could be observed. Say there's an electron floating around in space; we could say there's a 20% chance of it being in one location, a 50% chance of it being in another, and yet a 30% chance of it being in a third. Once we observe the electron as being in some place, our universe branches off into three separate universes, each with the electron in a different location. The probabilities, however, would actually be on a continuous distribution, not discrete values because -- as Sean Carroll, who explained the "many world" theory, made painfully clear -- the universe is one giant wave function. What that means is painfully unclear.

Side Note: space and matter are a lot more fungible and less fundamental than most of us think, so even referring to the universe's "wave function" is probably letting myself be intellectually boxed in by my own misguided language. Wow, my post-structuralist humanities professor would love this. Next time on Joe butchering complex explanations: the philosophy of Jacques Derrida!

Anyway, you can listen to a much, much better explanation of the many-worlds theory on the Ezra Klein Show (aka my favorite podcast) here. I highly recommend it.

No matter how little I understand it, I love learning and thinking about space, time, quantum physics, and all the crazy theories that try to describe our universe because...

  1. These concepts don't just make up me, my brain, and my world.
  2. They answer questions that existed before anyone was around to ask them. I don't believe in a traditional God... but knowing something small about the movements of the universe feels like communing with an eternal power. It's the closest I can get to significance, to meaning.
  3. They expose how misguiding our perceptions can be.

How do you move forward when everything you think you know is at odds with everyone who knows? How do you try to see your world differently? To expose yourself to all the pieces of it that the neurons in your brain try to keep you from seeing? The pieces you fear you'll never see.

That's the part I find most exciting about mind-blowing theories like many-worlds. Not the shattering of the puzzle, but how you try to put it back together again, differently.
