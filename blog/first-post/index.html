Why?

written by Joe Berusch on 2020-05-13

When I announced my intent to make a podcast, my sister sent me an article about how now -- in the days of quarantine -- is not the time to start your podcast. No one wants to hear it.

So I decided to start a blog instead.

I had a million things I wanted to write about every day and I couldn't decide where to start. Then, I read David Brooks' commencement speech in the Atlantic: A Commencement Address Too Honest to Deliver in Person. The article has terrific advice, and the piece I latched onto most was about learning after college.

Graduation day is a good day to step back and reflect on all the things you’ve learned during college. It’s also a good day to step back and reflect on all the ways your college failed you, on the pieces of your education this place should have given you but didn’t. You’re going to have to learn these things on your own.

Then, Brooks describes defining our mind by its upper limits. And how we expand our mind by raising that limit.

The theory of maximum taste says that each person’s mind is defined by its upper limit—the best that it habitually consumes and is capable of consuming.

...

In college, you get assigned hard things. You’re taught to look at paintings and think about science in challenging ways. After college, most of us resolve to keep doing this kind of thing, but we’re busy and our brains are tired at the end of the day. Months and years go by. We get caught up in stuff, settle for consuming Twitter and, frankly, journalism. Our maximum taste shrinks. Have you ever noticed that 70 percent of the people you know are more boring at 30 than they were at 20?

Melodramatic though it may seem, the final question struck fear into my heart. I never want to become boring.

So, this blog is me trying to maintain a high upper limit, to not be boring. Because you have to be interested in order to be interesting, I'll share my progress in learning something new every day.

Maybe some days I'll learn 10 things, others I'll learn 0. Maybe I'll learn things that aren't easy to communicate in a short post. Maybe I'll get really excited about an idea of my own and write about that, instead of the thing I learned that inspired it. Maybe none of the posts will be interesting to read at all. Who knows, I'm figuring it out as I go along.
