Thinking 50 Moves Ahead

written by Joe Berusch on 2020-05-15

Today I learned how insanely complicated the human brain is.

After yesterday's fun with quantum mechanics and the many-worlds theory, I picked an episode of Sean Carroll's podcast to accompany my walk through scenic Hyde Park. And, in a conversation with Stuart Russell, a legendary AI researched, my mind was blown yet again!

Russell brought up AlphaGo, the news-making AI program that beat world champ Lee Sedol at the game Go. There's a documentary about it on Netflix called AlphaGo -- I highly, highly recommend. The victory over Lee Sedol made major waves because, of course, it represented yet another complex challenge at which machines can beat humans. The coolest part though, was how AlphaGo beat Sedol. AlphaGo played a move that shocked Go experts and commentators around the world, all of whom declared the move idiotic. The move, however, turned out later in the game to be a linchpin in the machine's defeat of Sedol. When making the move, AlphaGo analyzed the likelihood an expert Go player would make the same move and found it to be very, very unlikely. It made the move anyway. Calling the wisdom of an entire field of experts and raising them a better, more advanced understanding of the game? Some call that creativity.

I let myself get sidetracked too easily. It's because AlphaGo is so darn cool. Anyway, Stuart Russell brought up AlphaGo to illustrate the complexity of current artificial intelligence compared to the complexity of the human brain. AlphaGo can think 50 moves ahead, which seems like a hell of a lot -- it certainly does to me. Lord knows that when I play my Dad in chess, anything beyond two moves in the future is completely opaque.

Russell, however, pointed out that a machine thinking 50 moves ahead in a game is our equivalent of thinking ahead 50 motor function executions (e.g. twitching a muscle, blinking an eye). Now that doesn't sound like much at all. We humans can perform hundreds or thousands of fine motor functions in a second, often subconsciously.

As bad as many of us (including and especially myself) are at thinking ahead in strategy games, we're masters of thinking ahead. We can think days, months, even years into the future, setting goals and making plans when a million million variables and decisions stand in our more immediate path. Not only can we think years ahead, but we can transition seamlessly between long-term and short-term thinking. We can switch from thoughts about the PhD we want to get in 6 years to the project we're working on for an undergrad class in no more than a heartbeat. We can balance thoughts of the physical shape we want our body to be in come Summer with thoughts of the food we're ordering today at the restaurant, all without batting an eye.

We humans are pretty damn impressive. All of us, regardless of any value societies may or may not give us, carry around with us the most sophisticated system of intelligence that's ever existed on this planet. I can see a million possibly implications of that consideration, but I haven't even begun to sort them out. So, for now, I won't try.

One Universe, Infinite

written by Joe Berusch on 2020-05-14

Today I learned that some physicists believe in a "many worlds" theory of quantum mechanics... and it's insane.

I'm going to butcher this theory, but here goes. These physicists believe that every time something is observed, the universe splits off into fragments, one for each of the possible ways that thing could be observed. Say there's an electron floating around in space; we could say there's a 20% chance of it being in one location, a 50% chance of it being in another, and yet a 30% chance of it being in a third. Once we observe the electron as being in some place, our universe branches off into three separate universes, each with the electron in a different location. The probabilities, however, would actually be on a continuous distribution, not discrete values because -- as Sean Carroll, who explained the "many world" theory, made painfully clear -- the universe is one giant wave function. What that means is painfully unclear.

Side Note: space and matter are a lot more fungible and less fundamental than most of us think, so even referring to the universe's "wave function" is probably letting myself be intellectually boxed in by my own misguided language. Wow, my post-structuralist humanities professor would love this. Next time on Joe butchering complex explanations: the philosophy of Jacques Derrida!

Anyway, you can listen to a much, much better explanation of the many-worlds theory on the Ezra Klein Show (aka my favorite podcast) here. I highly recommend it.

No matter how little I understand it, I love learning and thinking about space, time, quantum physics, and all the crazy theories that try to describe our universe because...

  1. These concepts don't just make up me, my brain, and my world.
  2. They answer questions that existed before anyone was around to ask them. I don't believe in a traditional God... but knowing something small about the movements of the universe feels like communing with an eternal power. It's the closest I can get to significance, to meaning.
  3. They expose how misguiding our perceptions can be.

How do you move forward when everything you think you know is at odds with everyone who knows? How do you try to see your world differently? To expose yourself to all the pieces of it that the neurons in your brain try to keep you from seeing? The pieces you fear you'll never see.

That's the part I find most exciting about mind-blowing theories like many-worlds. Not the shattering of the puzzle, but how you try to put it back together again, differently.

Why?

written by Joe Berusch on 2020-05-13

When I announced my intent to make a podcast, my sister sent me an article about how now -- in the days of quarantine -- is not the time to start your podcast. No one wants to hear it.

So I decided to start a blog instead.

I had a million things I wanted to write about every day and I couldn't decide where to start. Then, I read David Brooks' commencement speech in the Atlantic: A Commencement Address Too Honest to Deliver in Person. The article has terrific advice, and the piece I latched onto most was about learning after college.

Graduation day is a good day to step back and reflect on all the things you’ve learned during college. It’s also a good day to step back and reflect on all the ways your college failed you, on the pieces of your education this place should have given you but didn’t. You’re going to have to learn these things on your own.

Then, Brooks describes defining our mind by its upper limits. And how we expand our mind by raising that limit.

The theory of maximum taste says that each person’s mind is defined by its upper limit—the best that it habitually consumes and is capable of consuming.

...

In college, you get assigned hard things. You’re taught to look at paintings and think about science in challenging ways. After college, most of us resolve to keep doing this kind of thing, but we’re busy and our brains are tired at the end of the day. Months and years go by. We get caught up in stuff, settle for consuming Twitter and, frankly, journalism. Our maximum taste shrinks. Have you ever noticed that 70 percent of the people you know are more boring at 30 than they were at 20?

Melodramatic though it may seem, the final question struck fear into my heart. I never want to become boring.

So, this blog is me trying to maintain a high upper limit, to not be boring. Because you have to be interested in order to be interesting, I'll share my progress in learning something new every day.

Maybe some days I'll learn 10 things, others I'll learn 0. Maybe I'll learn things that aren't easy to communicate in a short post. Maybe I'll get really excited about an idea of my own and write about that, instead of the thing I learned that inspired it. Maybe none of the posts will be interesting to read at all. Who knows, I'm figuring it out as I go along.
